Taiwan's summers are turning deadlier
Blue line (left axis): nationwide heat-related illness rate per 10,000 — confirmed 2000–2018, projected 2019–2024  ·  Red bars (right axis): working-age hospitalisations, ages 19–64
Dashed section (2019–2024) projects the 2000–2018 confirmed linear trend forward; 2019–2024 nationwide published data not yet available. Working-age hospitalisations (right axis) are a subset of total HRI and use a different scale.
Taiwan recorded its hottest year since measurements began in 1897 in 2024 — an annual mean temperature of 24.97°C, 1.66°C above the century-long average. The health toll is tracking the mercury upward: a nationwide study of Taiwan's National Health Insurance database found the heat-related illness rate more than doubled between 2000 and 2018, from 1.76 to 4.17 per 10,000 people. In just the two years from 2022 to 2024, working-age hospital admissions surged a further 74%, from 1,622 to 2,829 cases — outpacing even the long-run trend. On an island sitting at the subtropical intersection of rising sea-surface temperatures and dense urban heat islands, the trajectory shows little sign of reversing.
Sources: BMC Public Health (2025) — Taiwan National Health Insurance Research Database (101,614 patients, 2000–2018); Taiwan Ministry of Health and Welfare (2025); Central Weather Administration (Taiwan CWA) | @enriqui
